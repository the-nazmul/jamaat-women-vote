Bangladesh National Election · Gender analysis

Women voted more for Jamaat

In Bangladesh, men and women often vote at separate centres. When one school building has both a men's centre and a women's centre, we can see how men and women in the same place voted. We found 2,453 such pairs. In them, one party did better with women than with men. It was not the BNP — it was Jamaat-e-Islami.

Overview · the election

The BNP won. Jamaat came second.

The BNP won the election with 211 seats and just over half of all votes. Jamaat-e-Islami came second with 67 seats. This story is not about who won. It is about something smaller inside the result: men and women voted differently.

Overview · the data

Only one in four centres shows gender

In most of Bangladesh, men and women vote at separate centres. These single-sex centres are only about a quarter of all centres. But they are the only places where we can tell if a vote came from a man or a woman. The other three-quarters are mixed, so they cannot tell us anything about gender.

Overview · the method

Comparing men and women in the same place

Comparing all women's centres with all men's centres can be misleading, because a women's centre may be in a different area than a men's centre. So we use a stricter method. We match each men's centre with the women's centre in the same building, and compare only inside these pairs. Same village, same street, same voters — the only difference is who voted. Every number in this story comes from these 2,453 matched pairs.

One important point: in a typical pair, men and women voted almost the same (the middle value is about 0). The advantage we show is real when you add everything up, but it is concentrated in a few areas — mainly Jamaat's strongholds in the south-west — not spread evenly across the country.

The main finding

The BNP is the same for men and women. Jamaat is not.

In the matched pairs, the BNP got almost the same vote share from men and women. Jamaat got 1.6 points more from women than from men.

Only Jamaat gained

Every other party lost ground among women

This is not simply because religious parties do better with women. The other Islamic party — Islami Andolan (Hand Fan) — actually did worse with women. So did the NCP and the others. Jamaat is the only party that got a higher share from women than from men at the same station.

Where it is strongest

In the south-west, women gave Jamaat a majority

The advantage is not the same everywhere. In the Jessore–Satkhira–Jhenaidah area — Jamaat's old stronghold — the party goes from 46% among men to 52% among women, a clear majority. That jump is more than six times bigger than in the rest of the country.

The biggest gaps

The 12 seats where women shifted most to Jamaat

In Jessore-1, Jamaat got 52% of the men's vote — and 65% of the women's vote. That is a gap of 13 points. The biggest gaps are all in the south-western districts.

All seats at once

Each dot is one seat. The bottom line shows Jamaat's share among men; the side line shows its share among women. A dot above the diagonal means Jamaat did better with women. Most dots sit just above the line — a small gap, not a big one. The gap grows on the right side, in Jamaat's strong seats.

District ranking

The women's advantage, district by district

Green bars are districts where Jamaat did better with women. The south-western districts are at the top. Only a few districts — mostly in Chattogram and the north — go the other way.

One more thing

Fewer women voted — but still chose Jamaat more

Women's turnout was almost 5 points lower than men's. So Jamaat's advantage with women is not just because more women voted. Even though fewer women voted, they still gave Jamaat a higher share. This is about choice, not turnout.

The full result

Two winners, two different kinds of wins

Leave gender aside for a moment. The BNP won a big majority — 211 seats to Jamaat's 67 — with just over half the vote. But the two parties won in very different ways.

Big wins vs close wins

The BNP wins by a lot. Jamaat wins by a little.

Where the BNP wins, it wins by 30 points on average. Where Jamaat wins, it wins by only 13 points — and 19 of its 67 seats were won by less than 5 points. In these close seats, a few points from women can decide the winner.

The key point

If only women voted, Jamaat would win more seats

Count each seat using its matched pairs only. With men's votes, Jamaat leads in 46 seats. With women's votes, it leads in 55. In 11 seats, men choose the BNP but women choose Jamaat — and only 2 seats go the other way. Women do not change who runs the country, but they clearly make Jamaat's group bigger.

Where women mattered most

Women helped Jamaat most in the seats it won

This is the clearest sign. Across all seats, women gave Jamaat 1.4 points more. But in the seats Jamaat actually won, women gave it 3.7 points more — more than two and a half times as much — and helped Jamaat in 82% of them. In those seats, women moved Jamaat from 49% among men to a 53% majority. Women's support is strongest exactly where Jamaat won.

In short

Key points

  • A fair comparison — same building, men vs women. We used 2,453 paired centres, so the gender gap is not just about location.
  • Jamaat is the only big party that gained among women — +1.6 points. The BNP stayed the same, and every other party did worse.
  • This is about Jamaat, not religious parties in general. The other Islamic party, Islami Andolan, did worse with women.
  • Strong in some places, not everywhere. In a typical seat the gap is about 0. The advantage comes from the south-west, where women push Jamaat above 50% (+5.7 points, vs +0.9 elsewhere).
  • Fewer women voted, but they still chose Jamaat more. So this is about choice, not turnout.
  • The BNP won, but in a different way. 211 seats to Jamaat's 67 — the BNP by 30 points on average, Jamaat by only 13 (19 of its wins were under 5 points).
  • Women make Jamaat's group bigger. With women's votes only, Jamaat leads in 55 seats vs 46 with men's — and 11 seats move from the BNP to Jamaat.
  • Women mattered most where Jamaat won. In its won seats, women gave +3.7 points — more than 2½ times the average — turning 49% among men into a 53% majority among women.
